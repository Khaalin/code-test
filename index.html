Platypus

Fan Facts

  • Platypuses don't have stomachs. They have a gullet that connects directly to their intestines, like some other animals.
  • A platypus's bill has thousands of cells that allow it to detect electric fields, giving it a "sixth sense" for hunting.
  • The platypus is a monotreme, meaning "single hole" in Greek, referring to its cloaca that serves as the exit for bodily wastes and eggs/sperm.
  • Female platypuses nurse their young without nipples, releasing milk through abdominal gland ducts for the babies to lap up.
  • Male platypuses have venomous spurs on their hind legs used for fending off rivals during mating season.
  • Platypuses have webbed feet with retractable webbing to aid swimming but expose claws on land.
  • When the first platypus specimen reached England, scientists thought it was an elaborate hoax.
  • Platypuses ingest gravel to use as makeshift teeth for grinding food.
  • A platypus uses its tail mainly for fat storage, but females also use it to incubate eggs.
  • The platypus is considered "near-threatened" due to habitat loss and water pollution impacting its freshwater environments.
  • Platypuses are protected by Australian law, with heavy penalties for capturing or killing them outside of approved research.
  • They're protected by Australian law. If you see a wild platypus, you cannot capture or kill it except for approved scientific research, with major fines for violations.
